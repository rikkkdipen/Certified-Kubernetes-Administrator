CKA: KUBE-PROXY — QUESTIONS & ANSWERS (Beginner → Expert)
========================================================

BEGINNER (FOUNDATION)
---------------------

1) Q: What is kube-proxy?
   A: kube-proxy is a node-level networking component that enables Kubernetes Services by
      routing Service traffic to Pod endpoints.

2) Q: Why do we need kube-proxy if Pods already have IPs?
   A: Pod IPs change. Services provide a stable virtual IP/name, and kube-proxy maps
      Service IP/port to the current Pod IPs/ports.

3) Q: Where does kube-proxy run?
   A: Usually as a DaemonSet in kube-system, meaning one pod per node.

4) Q: Does kube-proxy assign Pod IPs?
   A: No. The CNI plugin assigns Pod IPs and sets up pod networking.

INTERMEDIATE (CKA PRACTICAL)
----------------------------

5) Q: What resources does kube-proxy watch to build routing rules?
   A: Services and Endpoints/EndpointSlices (via kube-apiserver).

6) Q: What are kube-proxy modes?
   A:
   - iptables mode: creates iptables NAT rules for service routing.
   - IPVS mode: uses Linux IPVS for service load balancing.

7) Q: A Service exists but "kubectl get endpoints" shows none. What does it mean?
   A: Usually:
   - Service selector does not match pod labels, OR
   - Pods are not Ready (readiness failing), so they aren’t added as endpoints.

8) Q: Pods are Running and reachable via Pod IP, but Service ClusterIP is not working. What do you check?
   A:
   - Endpoints/EndpointSlices are populated correctly
   - kube-proxy is running on nodes
   - kube-proxy logs and service rules (iptables/ipvs)

9) Q: How do you quickly verify kube-proxy is running?
   A:
     kubectl -n kube-system get pods -o wide | grep kube-proxy
   or
     kubectl -n kube-system get ds kube-proxy

10) Q: What is the relation between readiness and Service endpoints?
    A: Only Ready pods are typically added as service endpoints. If readiness is failing,
       Service may have no endpoints or fewer endpoints.

EXPERT (TROUBLESHOOTING FLOW)
-----------------------------

11) Q: Explain the flow: Client -> Service -> Pod (where kube-proxy fits)
    A:
    - Client sends traffic to Service ClusterIP:Port (or NodeIP:NodePort).
    - kube-proxy uses iptables/IPVS rules on the node to NAT/forward traffic
      to one of the backend Pod IPs (endpoints).
    - Return traffic flows back normally through networking.

12) Q: Difference between CNI networking and kube-proxy responsibility?
    A:
    - CNI: gives pods IPs and enables pod-to-pod networking.
    - kube-proxy: implements Service virtual IP and load-balancing to pod endpoints.

13) Q: NodePort not working on one node but works on another. What might be wrong?
    A:
    - kube-proxy on the failing node might be unhealthy/not running.
    - node firewall rules or iptables/ipvs rules may differ.
    - Check kube-proxy pod on that node and its logs.

14) Q: What kube-proxy-related checks are common in CKA?
    A:
    - Validate service selectors/labels:
      kubectl describe svc <svc>
      kubectl get pods --show-labels
    - Validate endpoints:
      kubectl get endpoints <svc>
      kubectl get endpointslices
    - Check kube-proxy:
      kubectl -n kube-system get pods -o wide | grep kube-proxy
      kubectl -n kube-system logs <kube-proxy-pod>

15) Q: In one line, how do you decide if it’s kube-proxy vs app issue?
    A:
    - If Pod IP works but Service IP/NodePort fails: likely kube-proxy/service/endpoints.
    - If Pod IP itself fails: likely app/container/CNI/networking.
